Walking along Pier 39, the familiar scent of saltwater wafted in on the ocean breeze. The “Magowan’s Infinite Mirror Maze" sign, in bright colors, caught my attention. As I stood staring at the entrance, a flurry of emotions clouded my head. My unease around mirrors as a child and my fascination for the maze battled, and curiosity won. I entered the maze with my sister and started exploring.
​At this point, I should divulge that mirrors have always made me uneasy, especially in middle school. Looking into the mirror every morning before school was difficult because it reflected things about myself that I could not change. My preconceived negative opinions about my appearance always seemed to be validated by my bathroom mirror.  As the only Sri Lankan student in my high school and the only one who spoke Sinhalese as a second language, I was constantly seeking the elusive feeling of belonging in a predominantly ethnically different community.
​Next, I was met by my own reflection and suddenly found myself back in a classroom in middle school, surrounded by my peers. The room was filled with the smell of old books, and conversations flowed around me without ever including me. I felt flushed and nervous, unsure how to join in. Before I could find the courage to speak, the conversation had already moved on. Despite my quiet nature, I always wanted to share my voice and my opinions, but it often felt as though the world kept moving forward without me.
​That feeling of being left behind stayed with me, and it made me hesitant to speak up in new spaces until speech and debate forced me to confront it directly.  Walking farther along the path, I saw another reflection, this one capturing the moment that changed my life. The aroma of coffee and donuts greeted me as I stepped into my first speech and debate tournament. My teacher, Mr. Pritchard, standing at a towering six feet tall, felt less like a coach and more like a “final boss” in a video game, one guarding not failure, but my potential as a speaker.
​
As I stood on stage facing the judge, whose eyes seemed practically to burn a hole through me, I clenched my paper tightly as my leg bounced uncontrollably. When I finally began speaking, my mind went blank. I rushed through my speech in a voice so quiet it could barely be heard. Only after I finished did I realize, to my embarrassment, that I had spent the entire speech with my back turned to the judge. At that moment, I felt small and defeated. Looking back now, though, I can only laugh at the memory.
​
That first performance taught me that failure was part of the process of growth in speech and debate, as well as the value of accepting mistakes and moving on. Eventually, I signed up for more events and continued to compete in Extemporaneous Speaking and Impromptu, where I learned to think on my feet. I practiced for long hours and  learned how to argue a point convincingly on short notice and continuously challenge myself with confidence. Not surprisingly, the cheat sheet also disappeared as I moved through high school, eventually leading the middle school group and mentoring its members.
​
As I look into the next mirror, I envision a younger teammate beside me. She reminds me of my older self, quiet and nervous to speak up. I ask her, “Do you really want to do this…? Speech and debate, I mean.” She nods but remains quiet.
I tell her, “Then yell as loud as you can.” She gives me a confused look and says, “I can’t.”
“We’ll do it together. Ready? On my count.”
For the rest of the practice, she and I yell together. We laugh awkwardly between each shout, noticing the weird looks we receive. As she keeps yelling, I realize that her voice is growing stronger than mine. once struggled to use my own voice—now I help someone else find hers. 

As a mentor for the middle school speech and debate team every Friday after school, I learnt to be a patient leader and eventually would lead my mentees to win some of the competitions we attended
​
Outside of competition, I began working with adolescents with Down syndrome at Gigi’s Playhouse. Dante greeted me each time as if no time had passed. I expected to feel like I was “helping,” but instead I found myself being welcomed into his world with an ease I didn’t expect.  I am not naturally a touchy person, but his hugs made me feel unexpectedly welcome. He would talk endlessly about video games and music with the same enthusiasm each visit, and consider me family. After spending much of my life searching for belonging, I began to understand something important: community is not something you find. It is something you build by showing up consistently, even when it feels small. I found community at school and in my area by starting my own club and joining the Placer Youth Commission to advocate for the youth in my community.
​
I finally saw a light at the end of the maze. It had to be the exit. As I turned, I caught a glimpse of myself. Instead of my seeing another memory, my reflection looked back at me. Confident as a future leader in society who would advocate for those in need. For the first time in years, I felt comfortable with myself.
​
I realized the reflection never needed to change.
